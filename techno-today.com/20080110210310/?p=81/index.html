Will patents kill VoIP technology?

January 1, 2007 |

The successes of many technologies depend on patents. A technology will probably have greatest success if it can be implemented royalty free. Take for example the difference in popularity between CDMA and GSM/GPRS. CDMA although a superior technology has taken a back seat because of the huge licensing fees that companies owe to Qualcomm. Given this is the case, a hot technology area these days is Voice over IP (VoIP), which is basically a technology that allows making voice calls over the internet. According to Business Week magazine,

The Patent & Trademark Office is approving patents aplenty for Internet-based calling. On Nov. 14 alone, it handed out a patent on what IBM calls a “conversations computing system,” and granted chipmaker Intel a patent for a computer-based phone “eliminating the need for a telephone set.”

By issuing such trivial patents to technology giants like IBM and Intel, the Patent System in the US is seriously preventing this technology from emerging. Now, any company that implements VoIP products will have to pay royalty charges to IBM and Intel for what I think are fairly trivial patents. Such patents are not new. Infact, Research In Motion Inc had to pay a whopping $600+ million because wireless “push” email through the use of a server was patented by a patent troll company (NTP). One hopes these patents don’t kill a extremely hot technology area which promises to revolutionize telephone system.
