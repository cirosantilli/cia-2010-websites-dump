No DRM or More DRM?

February 18, 2007 | Leave a Comment

The issue of the use of Digital Rights Management (DRM) is the most passionate debate that is going on between the technology and music Industry. Ever since Steve Job’s open letter to CEO(s) of music industry last week, this issue has become a hot topic. So let’s start off by looking at what’s at stake and how we got into this situation.

DRM was basically technology industry response to issues concerning piracy. This was mainly added in response to concerns from music industry (mainly Disney, Viacom, Universal, and Sony). However, there were no standards, and so different vendors have implemented different DRMs. For example, Apple has used its own proprietary standard called FairPlay. Microsoft and allies have used PlaysForSure. Microsoft has also implemented another version called PlayReady. Recently, Real also implemented its own proprietary standard. These varying implementations have benefited Apple greatly, since it tied up Apple users of iPod music player to their iTunes online music store.

However, trouble started brewing when European countries decided to get rid of Apple’s monopoly by requiring Apple to license its FairPlay DRM to other vendors so they can have a level playing field. However, Apple tried to deflect criticism by saying that it was only trying to protect music industry and by licensing DRM to other vendors it will lead to greater piracy. Finally, Steve Jobs felt compelled enough to respond to these accusations by the European countries through this open letter.

I personally think the Europeans are right to get rid of Apple’s monopoly in this segment. I hope this leads to all technology and music companies getting together to draft a standard that could be universally implemented across the board so that we have just one DRM standard. However, the license of this format should be only provided to legitimate companies, for otherwise it would fall into the hands of hackers who could then illegally rip music. The other alternative, if to follow Job’s suggestion and discard DRM all together, but this will lead consumers paying more for their music. But I have a feeling that RIAA and music industry are never going to accept this proposal.

A worrying trend?

February 11, 2007 | 4 Comments

When Intel announced an 80 core prototype chip a few days back, one of the most well know computer architects David A. Patterson said the following,

“If we can figure out how to program thousands of cores on a chip, the future looks rosy, If we can’t figure it out, then things look dark.”

It really puts into question how far software lags behind hardware in the ability to parallelize computations. Parallelism at the software level has been lagging and this will limit the efficiency of multi-core machines that we are likely to see in the coming years. Perhaps one of the most well known chip today that faces this problem is the cell micro-processor found on PS3 gaming machines. A heterogeneous 8 (well technically 9 cores, 1 is just used for testing purposes) core machine which goes into great effort to hide memory latency suffers greatly from the fact that programmers aren’t able to develop high performance games that it is capable of handling. The real problem according to many experts is the fact that no solution looks in sight for this problem and we may well be stuck with too many cores, and very little parallelism to utilize it.

Vodafone enters Indian market

February 11, 2007 | Leave a Comment

As most analyst expected, Vodafone has brought a majority 67% stake in Hutch-Essar. Vodafone’s bid was superior to those of Reliance, and consortium led by the Hindujas. Vodafone was really interested to get into the fast growing Indian mobile market, which is likely to be the 2nd largest market by the end of next year. Vodafone already owns part of Bharati, but it doesn’t have a controlling stake. However, Bharati did not oppose Vodafone’s bid.

The price that Vodafone payed to get a controlling stake has surprised many invested who feel that Vodafone’s bid is far too aggressive. However, Vodafone’s CEO Arun Sarin doesn’t seem very worried. The biggest loser in the whole episode is Anil Ambani’s Reliance Communications. Reliance’s foray into the GSM market is now going to be delayed and the market is likely to punish Reliance for it at least in the short run.

Windows Mobile 6.0 released

February 8, 2007 | Leave a Comment

The much anticipated Windows Mobile 6.0 OS (code-name ‘Crossbow’) released yesterday. The OS supports tight integration with Microsoft’s Live.com service, improved stability and reliability, and some minor tweaks and newer version of mobile application services (like new .NET Compact Framework, SQL Server etc). The complete set of features according to another online portal Hive.net blog are as follows,

- Email in Rich HTML Format.
- Live links to SharePoint sites.
- Windows Live for Mobile included in Windows Mobile 6.
- New Security features such as remote wiping capabilities.
- Enhanced Vista synchronization through Windows Mobile Device Center.
- Calendar ribbon gives you your important appoints quickly.
- .NET Compact Framework and SQL Server built in to Windows Mobile 6.

    The new Windows Mobile theme is kind jazzy. It’s modeled on the lines of the Live.com. But its really good to see Microsoft is making some progress with this release in integrating its services together. I guess Vista’s next version will follow the same path as Windows Mobile.

    Billing Wireless Data!

    February 4, 2007 | 1 Comment

    I wanted to bring to attention a crucial question that wireless carriers need to decide over the next few years: How to bill wireless data? The question may seem odd at first, and looks fairly straightforward. But it’s really not. Traditionally, wireless carriers have been billing customers on per-minute usage. This model worked extremely well for phone calls. But ‘data’ is completely different service. There are 2 ways to bill it currently: (1) per megabyte usage (2) monthly flat fees.

    Currently, most carriers bill customers on per megabyte usage. This model has many advantages and is extremely lucrative for the carriers from the business point of view. It also requires wireless hand-held manufacturers like Palm, Research In Motion, Motorola, and Nokia to be extremely cautions/efficient in their data transfer. This means all the services are designed to be fundamentally efficient in terms of bandwidth usage. However, the down side to this model is per megabyte usage really can’t be ascertained properly. For example, any reliable wireless protocol requires data be re-transmitted when lost. Most of the time these loses comes from congestion in the network. So customers pay a heavy price for congested networks that are provided by the carriers themselves. This is not desirable.

    The second billing model is flat fees. Currently, I don’t know any major carriers that have adopted this scheme. The ‘wired’ broadband Internet has worked in this model. The problem with using this model in a wireless environment is that the effective bandwidth of a wireless network is far less and most of the high speed networks (3G and 4G) are still not widely deployed. So, with wireless data services growing over the next few years, it will be interesting to see how this plays out. Handheld manufacturers will be affected by the path the wireless network carriers choose to adopt. From customers point of view ‘flat fee’ model works best. But with carriers using to per megabyte/min billing, it is unlikely that they will adopt a new model in a hurry…
